RK 02 · The opportunity
The opportunity

What gets unlockedby adding a checkout.

Online ordering isn't just convenience. For a small bakery with limited weekend capacity, it changes how the business works.

i.

Pre-orders fill the weekend before it starts.

Right now a tiramisu order means a DM thread, a back-and-forth on size and pickup, and a Venmo or Zelle handoff. Each one takes minutes. With a checkout, that's a 30-second customer flow and a confirmed order in your inbox before you've even started baking Friday night.

ii.

Capacity stops being a guessing game.

Cap each weekend at the number of tiramisus you can actually make. The site sells out, the message is automatic ("see you next weekend"), and you're never overcommitted at 11pm Saturday night.

iii.

The Mr Rabbit model becomes repeatable.

That collab is a wholesale arrangement in disguise. With the right backend, the next coffee shop that wants in places their order through a portal — bulk pricing, net-30 invoicing, all of it. The infrastructure pays for itself the second collab.

iv.

The newsletter has something to actually drive to.

Right now "subscribe for updates" is a soft ask. With a shop, every newsletter is a sales tool — "this weekend's flavours are live, three slots left, link below." That's the difference between a list and a list that pays for itself.

That said — none of this is necessary. The site as it stands works. This is an upgrade, not a fix. Three paths follow, one of which is "do less." Whichever fits, fits.

RK 04 · The smart play
My honest recommendation

The smart play Build to demand, not to imagination.

If I were spending my own money, I wouldn't pay for Path 03 today. I'd pay for Path 02, run it two months, then add only what demand actually shows up for. Here's why — and the math.

i.

Validate first.

Subscriptions, wholesale, loyalty — great if anyone uses them. You don't know yet what your customers actually want. Two months of real signal beats six months of guessing.

ii.

Cash flow.

$1,400 is easier to find than $3,000 today. Phase III upgrades pay for themselves out of Phase II revenue — you're never out-of-pocket on a feature you can't afford.

iii.

Operational sanity.

Running a shop AND a subscription AND a wholesale portal day one is too many new systems for one baker. Master one. Get comfortable. Then layer the next.

RK 05 · How we choose
How we choose

Twelve questions, twenty minutes.

The right path depends entirely on your answers. We can walk through these together at the meeting — your answers will collapse three paths to one.

  1. In a typical week, how many orders do you take? Mostly DMs, mostly word-of-mouth?
    If the answer is "10+ a week," Path 02 pays for itself fast. If it's "2-3," Path 01 is plenty.
  2. What's the average order? Is it $14 tiramisus or $200 custom cakes?
    Higher AOV means BNPL matters less; standing items at $14 means Apple Pay matters a lot.
  3. What share of orders are standing items vs custom cakes? Roughly.
    Custom-heavy means the inquiry form already does most of the work. Standing-heavy = real shop.
  4. Have you ever turned someone away because you ran out of capacity? How often?
    If yes — capacity caps in the shop are a sanity-saver, not a luxury.
  5. Have you ever lost an order because the customer ghosted before paying?
    Online checkout solves this. The order exists when the money does.
  6. Is Mr Rabbit a one-off, or do you want more wholesale collabs over time?
    "Yes, more" pushes us toward Path 03's wholesale portal.
  7. Have you considered a subscription product? "Tiramisu of the Month" or similar?
    Recurring revenue changes the maths on everything. Path 03 territory.
  8. Pickup-only forever, or delivery eventually?
    Affects shipping logic, packaging, time-of-day windows.
  9. Where do you see this in a year — same scale, 2x, 5x?
    Build for tomorrow's scale, not today's. Within reason.
  10. How much time can you put into managing the system each week?
    If "very little," the Airtable / Care Plan layer becomes essential.
  11. Do you have an LLC and a separate business bank account?
    Stripe needs one or the other. We can set this up if you don't.
  12. Are you registered to charge sales tax in Pennsylvania?
    Required for online food sales over a certain threshold. We can register you in 30 minutes.
